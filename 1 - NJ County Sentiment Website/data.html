Data and Findings

The information below consists of county geocode specifics, a Tableau visualization depicting the number of cases during the Tweet collection period, and a findings menu containing statistical analysis from SPSS, choropleth (heat) maps from R, and individual county sentiment analysis.

# County Geocode Area Radius


After SPSS bivariate correlation and linear regression modeling, several key insights were revealed (To view any of the SPSS results in greater detail, click on any of the images above to expand.). Through bivariate correlation, both positive and negative tweet sentiments maintained a significance (p) value greater than the established 0.05 critical value, indicating weak evidence against the null hypothesis, thus failing to reject the null hypothesis. In other words, these values also indicate greater coincidence over correlative genuineness. Similarly, the correlation (r) values of both positive and negative tweet sentiment counts indicate a more scattered and less linear-like relationship. Although, its positive direction indicates that the dependent ‘case’ variable increases as the independent sentiment variables increase. Approaching the r-square value, the lower value indicates that tweet sentiments predict only 11.4% of the variance of the dependent variable (number of cases), indicating a weaker (more scattered) fit of the regression line. Given the positive skew of the distribution plot, it appears that the method’s weakness - the number of tweets collected in each county - resulted in lower bounds affecting the overall skew of the distribution. This can be accredited to both potential limitations in the Twitter search methodology used, in addition to a lack of COVID-19, or “coronavirus” related tweets in a given county.


The above choropleth maps (better known as heat maps) depict the positive and negative tweet sentiment count densities by county. Together, these maps reveal two interesting occurrences. In approaching the positive tweet sentiments choropleth map, the greatest count density is primarily evident in the southern region of the state. Examining the Tableau county case visualization above, this positive sentiment is consistent with a smaller number of confirmed cases in the same subregion. Peculiarly, the negative sentiment counts are also largely concentrated in a similar subregion, apart from a few counties in northern regions of the state. This finding could be consistent with some of the near-equal sentiment ratios uncovered in the individual county analysis below. In such instances, certain counties exhibited a near identical positive and negative tweet sentiment count from the tweet sample in that area. This could indicate neutral attitudes or disagreements among the populations in these counties, thereby explaining the coexistence of the opposing densities depicted in the choropleth maps. Another possibility is that this finding is representative of the fairly uneven population distribution in the state. Much of New Jersey’s population is concentrated in the north, thus explaining the increased outbreak severity in the northern counties. The southern region of the state is far less populated in comparison, but contains the greatest density of COVID-19 tweet sentiments. To better test this hypothesis, additional tweet collection over a longer period in the COVID-19 pandemic would be required.
